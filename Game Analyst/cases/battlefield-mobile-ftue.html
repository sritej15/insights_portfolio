N° 01 — Case study
Battlefield Mobile · FTUE redesign

The FTUE checklist that shipped on paper but not in play

Rebuilding the first hour of a Battlefield title around player experience, not just mechanics.

StudioEA · Industrial Toys
RoleEmbedded player analyst
StagePre-launch
OutcomeFull FTUE redesign shipped
Context

Battlefield Mobile, pre-launch. FTUE put the player on Grand Bazaar, walked them through aim, shoot, ammo pickup, traversal, and tank driving, then dropped them into a nerfed bot match to practice. On paper the FTUE covered the right mechanics. Longitudinal playtests and internal feedback said the moment-to-moment experience was flat.

Signal

Three drop points surfaced in the telemetry: mid-FTUE (before the rocket launcher step), post-FTUE (before entering the bot match), and a few minutes into the first bot match itself. Completion rate looked acceptable. Retention at each transition didn't.

Fig 1 — Three drop points across FTUE and first bot match. Each required a different read.
Method

I worked each drop against telemetry and longitudinal playtest feedback. Drop 1, mid-FTUE: players completed the mechanics content and left before picking up the rocket launcher, the step that was meant to deliver Battlefield destruction VFX. The qualitative theme was that the tutorial felt mechanical and hand-holdy. Drop 2, post-FTUE: players finished the tutorial but didn't enter the bot match. They had will, not motivation. Drop 3, inside the match: players who entered quit within a few minutes. The bot tuning was fine. The experience leading up to it hadn't made them want to be there.

Across all three, the pattern synthesized to one diagnosis: the moment-to-moment experience was flat, too linear, and not recognizably Battlefield.

Insight
Teaching a player how to aim and move is not the same as showing them why Battlefield is Battlefield.

The FTUE was completing its job as a tutorial and failing its job as a first impression. The destruction, the scale, the squad feel, the reasons someone picks up this game, were absent from the hour that decided whether they kept playing. The fix couldn't be tuning the existing FTUE. The FTUE needed to be rebuilt around the experience, not the checklist.

Fig 2 — The redesigned FTUE opened with a cutscene on a different map, delivered destruction VFX from a helicopter, and kept the player company with a buddy and VO throughout.
Action & outcome
  1. Presented the three-drop diagnosis to design and UX with telemetry and qualitative feedback side by side, recommending the FTUE be rebuilt around player experience, not mechanics coverage.
  2. The team agreed the hypothesis held and committed to a full FTUE redesign. I stayed involved as the embedded analyst voice, validating changes against the original drop points.
  3. The shipped redesign opened with a cutscene on a different map, put the player in a helicopter with a buddy on VO, delivered destruction VFX via a rocket fired from the air, and arrived at the bot match with the game's identity already established. FTUE completion, transition-to-first-match, loadout engagement, and deeper-level progression all improved against pre-change baselines.
