N° 04 — Case study
Tomb Raider · Vendor quality framework

QX Scale transfer: structured vendor feedback that upgraded milestone decisions

Turning vendor output into evidence that milestone reviewers could weight the same as internal findings.

StudioAmazon Games
RoleQuality lead · vendor partnership
Scope10–15 vendor engineers across studios
OutcomeFramework transferred & adopted
Context

Tomb Raider. I was managing 10–15 vendor test engineers across multiple external studios, with internal reviewers relying on their output at each milestone gate. Internally we ran a QX Scale — a gradient of quality signals with scope, severity, and context baked into each grade. Externally, the vendors didn't.

Signal

Vendor reports arrived as free-text observations. Things like "combat feels off in Zone 3" or "pacing slower than expected mid-act." Accurate, often exactly right, unusable at a milestone review. Reviewers couldn't tell how wide the problem was, how severe, or whether it was a systemic miss or one tester's taste. So the findings were deprioritized, not because they were wrong, but because they couldn't be weighted.

Fig 1 — The transfer didn't change what vendors saw. It changed what milestone reviewers could do with it.
Method

I transferred the internal QX Scale to the vendor pipeline as a practical toolkit, not a policy. It had to survive being used by people I didn't share a standup with.

The transfer had three parts. First, a shared rubric that defined each QX grade with concrete examples drawn from the Tomb Raider build, so "QX2" meant the same thing to a vendor engineer as it did to an internal reviewer. Second, a reporting template that forced every finding to carry area, grade, sample count, and a short evidence note — enough structure to compare across vendors and against internal reports. Third, a calibration cycle: early reports were reviewed with vendor leads, mis-graded items were discussed, and the rubric was tightened where the edges were soft. After two cycles, grading held within tolerance without my sitting in the room.

Insight
Vendors weren't producing worse signal. They were producing unstructured signal, and unstructured signal loses at a milestone review.

The fix wasn't to get more from the vendors. It was to transfer the same scaffolding internal reviewers used, so vendor findings arrived at the milestone in the same shape as internal ones and could be weighted against them fairly.

Action & outcome
  1. Built and delivered the QX Scale transfer — rubric, template, calibration cycle — to 10–15 vendor engineers across multiple studios.
  2. Ran two calibration passes with vendor leads, locked the rubric where edges were soft, and integrated the structured vendor reports into the internal milestone packet alongside first-party findings.
  3. At milestone reviews, vendor findings were weighted at parity with internal ones. Reviewers could see scope, severity, and corroboration on every line. Several vendor-surfaced issues that would have been deprioritized under free-text made it into the milestone action list and were addressed before the gate closed.
What this reinforced
The signal-to-action gap across studio boundaries is almost always a format problem, not a talent problem. Transfer the scaffolding, don't re-collect the evidence.
